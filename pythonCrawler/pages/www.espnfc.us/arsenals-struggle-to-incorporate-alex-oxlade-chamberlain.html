Alex Oxlade-Chamberlain's role at Arsenal a dilemma for Arsene Wenger

It was a minor footnote of the international break that Alex Oxlade-Chamberlain started both of England's Euro 2016 qualifiers, but it had significance for Arsenal. It means the midfielder now occupies a rare position in that he is a first-choice pick for his country and not his club, a situation that must give Arsene Wenger pause for thought.

For a manager who is erroneously said to distrust young, vibrant talents, the faith shown in Oxlade-Chamberlain by Roy Hodgson has been notable. As a teenager he started the opening match of Euro 2012 against France, and the only active midfielders to surpass his 21 appearances in Hodgson's 42 matches in charge of England are James Milner (30) and Jack Wilshere (23). England have also not lost a single game in which he has played.

At this point, it is impossible not to invite comparisons with his situation at Arsenal, where Oxlade-Chamberlain's grip on a first-team role is rather more tenuous, his status rather less assured. If everyone is fit at Emirates Stadium, there is currently little chance that a player who would walk into almost any other Premier League side will get a game.

That is not to say he has not had chances. When Alexis Sanchez missed the start of the season after his Copa America exertions, Oxlade-Chamberlain scored against Chelsea in the Community Shield and was Arsenal's brightest player in the defeat to West Ham, even if he was muscled far too easily off the ball by Mauro Zarate for their second goal. When Mesut Ozil missed the Newcastle game due to a minor injury, Oxlade-Chamberlain came in and forced the only goal of the game when his shot deflected in off Fabricio Coloccini.

In what has been a toothless start to the campaign for Arsenal, here is a player who indisputably makes things happen. But he is a victim of congestion and, more specifically, the delicate dilemma over what to do about Aaron Ramsey.

Ramsey's talent is so evident and near complete that he must play. But the paradox comes with the realisation that inserting him into the team compromises Arsenal in other areas. If he plays in midfield as he did in the first half against West Ham, then the well-functioning Francis Coquelin-Santi Cazorla partnership is broken up and Arsenal lose a measure of balance.

If Ramsey plays as a central playmaker (arguably his best position), it necessitates Mesut Ozil moving to the left and significantly inhibiting his influence as well as displacing Alexis Sanchez. By process of elimination, the best way Arsenal can use Ramsey in the system they currently employ is to play him on the right, where he has largely been a success.

Oxlade-Chamberlain is surely a superior and more natural option for the attacking midfield position on the right, the role he occupies for England, but to make the system work and accommodate their senior talents, Arsenal cannot play him. It is the classic "nice problem to have" for a football manager, but doesn't make it any easier to solve.

This situation could have been adapted from the pages of "Catch-22": if Arsenal want to play their best players, Oxlade-Chamberlain has to start, but if Oxlade-Chamberlain starts, Arsenal cannot play all their best players.

With England having switched back to a front three after using a 4-4-2 diamond formation at the start of the qualifying process, there are no such logical riddles at international level. It makes for a more comfortable existence.

"It is really nice to know that a manager has faith in you, which he definitely has," said Oxlade-Chamberlain of Hodgson before the double-header of the past week. "It gives you more confidence and security to go out there and perform. It's something I'm grateful for."

Only the most cynical would present this as a direct challenge to Wenger, but it is surely true that Hodgson has been a more committed cheerleader for the midfielder purely because he does not have the same wealth of options available to the Arsenal boss.

Politically, it's easier for Wenger to drop the young and genial Oxlade-Chamberlain than it is the more established and better remunerated talents of Ramsey, Ozil or Cazorla. But that does not disguise the fact that he now deserves the same opportunities at Arsenal as he is getting with England. His level of performance and huge potential demand as much.

In the same interview in which he had such fulsome praise for Hodgson, Oxlade-Chamberlain outlined the importance of the months ahead.

"I do feel it's a big season for me to kick on to my own expectations of where I want to be and where I want to push myself to," Oxlade-Chamberlain said. "I had that in mind in preseason and still now. I did feel really good, not with a point to prove, but I wanted to step up and perform as well as I can."

The impulse is understandable from a player who last season could barely even get off his sofa at times without being struck down by pain. And now, with a starting role at the Euros apparently within his grasp, he needs to play enough club matches to safeguard that international status and enjoy the defining season he aspires to.

It is a difficult balance for Wenger to strike. He has never been a manager to stand in the way of emergent talent but accommodating Oxlade-Chamberlain is a riddle that has no easy answer at present.
